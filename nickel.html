Nickel

Nickel is a metallic element with a silvery-white, shiny appearance. It is the fifth-most common element on earth and occurs extensively in the earth's crust and core. Nickel, along with iron, is also a common element in meteorites.

Nickel is used in batteries, including rechargeable nickel-cadmium batteries and nickel-metal hydride batteries used in hybrid vehicles.

Identity.

Nickel (atomic number 28) resembles iron (atomic number 26) in strength and toughness but is more like copper (atomic number 29) in resistance to oxidation and corrosion, a combination accounting for many of its applications. Nickel has high electrical and thermal conductivity.

Atomic Structure:

The nucleus consists of 28 protons (red) and 31 neutrons (orange). 28 electrons (white) successively occupy available electron shells (rings).

History.

For centuries, miners stumbled upon nickel-containing ores, mistaking them for valuable copper due to their similar appearance. This frustration even led to the element's name. "Kupfernickel," meaning "copper-nickel" in German, was a term laced with miners' annoyance at finding "deceptive little spirits" instead of their desired copper.

Fast forward to the 18th century, when Swedish chemist Axel Fredrik Cronstedt finally isolated nickel in 1751. Despite initial skepticism, further research confirmed its unique properties. Nickel's strength, resistance to corrosion, and ability to blend with other metals made it a valuable addition to the industrial toolbox.

Usage.

Nickel is used in batteries, including rechargeable nickel-cadmium batteries and nickel-metal hydride batteries used in hybrid vehicles.

  • Stainless steel:Nickel is a key ingredient in stainless steel, making it resistant to rust and corrosion. This makes it ideal for cookware, appliances, and architectural components.
  • Superalloys:These nickel-based alloys can withstand high temperatures and stresses, making them essential for jet engines, gas turbines, and other demanding applications.
  • Magnet alloys: Nickel is used in some types of magnets, such as those found in electric motors and loudspeakers.
Some of the benefits of using nickel are:
  • Nickel is a crucial element in lithium-ion batteries, powering electric vehicles and grid storage systems. This transition to cleaner energy sources helps combat climate change.
  • Nickel is highly recyclable, meaning a large portion of used nickel can be recovered and reused, minimizing waste and resource extraction.
  • Nickel is a key component in permanent magnets, essential for various technologies like electric motors, wind turbines, and medical imaging equipment.

Sources.

  • While not abundant, nickel can be found in various minerals like pentlandite and garnierite. These minerals are often found in deposits, which can then be mined to extract the nickel.
  • These potato-sized lumps on the ocean floor contain various metals, including nickel. However, harvesting them raises environmental concerns, so this is not a common source at present.
  • Traces of nickel can be found in some meteorites, offering clues about the element's presence in our solar system. Studying these extraterrestrial sources can help us understand the formation and evolution of planets.
